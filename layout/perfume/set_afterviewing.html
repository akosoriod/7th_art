SCHOOL SUPPLIES, A CAUSE OF DRUG ADDICTION

 
From the Spanish orgininal "Elementos escolares: una causa de drogadicción"
By: Magda Páez Torres, Unimedios
Abridged and adapted by: Elizabeth Rodriguez
Translated by: Diana Cartagena
 
Markers, erasers, glue, and spray paint are some of the products that, due to the solvents in them, are causing drug addiction in minors in the country. What is in appearance just a school supply may be the first step towards drug addiction.


The Toxicology Department Psychoactive Substances Research Group at Universidad Nacional de Colombia found that around 7 per cent of the child population with drug addiction problems started by inhaling the solvents present in school supplies such as erasers, spray paint, markers, and glue.


These supplies contain aromatic substances such as benzene and xylene - hydrocarbon by-products - which children take closer to their noses because of their pleasant smell. The result is hallucination episodes and addiction because their characteristics produce a sensation of inebriation and well-being that creates a sort of habit in the organism. At the beginning, the effect is sought unconsciously. Later, it becomes voluntary developing into the use of illegal drugs such as marihuana, cocaine and basuco (low grade cocaine).
Because of their lipophilic (similar to fat) and liposoluble (soluble in fat) nature, they easily reach the brain, which is rich in fat, and once there generate an impact on human health. According to Carlos Osorno, Chemical Engineer from Universidad Nacional de Colombia, besides being carcinogenic, benzene and xylene produce somnolence, headaches, and even dizziness.



Figures speak for themselves

In a first study (2001-2002) developed with 127 individuals undergoing a drug addiction rehabilitation process, the Head of the Toxicology Department Jairo Tellez found out
that 55 per cent of the individuals had occasionally inhaled glue, and 6 per cent had inhaled substances such as thinner, varsol, and other solvents present in school supplies.


MSc in Toxicology Professor Francisco Sánchez conducted another study (2006) which showed that out of 78 minors in a drug addiction rehabilitation program, 13 per cent inhaled solvent substances regularly. In the same way, researcher William Quevedo found that out of 343 individuals in a rehabilitation program (2007), 17 per cent had used inhalable substances different from gasoline and industrial glues.


Ulises Cruz, from the Colombian Therapeutic Community, states that almost 100 per cent of the population he treats started using drugs between ages 12 and 13, which makes their recovery much more complex. "When consumption starts later in life (after age 18), it is easier for a person to quit their addiction. Early consumption start makes it more difficult to break dependency", he explained.
