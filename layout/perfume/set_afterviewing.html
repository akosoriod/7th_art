Markers, erasers, glue, and spray paint are some of the products that, due to the solvents in them, are causing drug addiction in minors in the country. What is in appearance just a school supply may be the first step towards drug addiction.

From the Spanish original “Elementos escolares: una causa de drogadicción” by Magda Páez Torres, Unimedios Abridged and adapted by Elizabeth Rodriguez Translated by Diana Cartagena

The Toxicology Department Psychoactive Substances Research Group at Universidad Nacional de Colombia found that around 7 per cent of the child population with drug addiction problems started by inhaling the solvents present in school supplies such as erasers, spray paint, markers, and glue. These supplies contain aromatic substances such as benzene and xylene – hydrocarbon by-products – which children take closer to their noses because of their pleasant smell. The result is hallucination episodes and addiction because their characteristics produce a sensation of inebriation and well-being that creates a sort of habit in the organism. At the beginning, the effect is sought unconsciously. Toxicology Professor Francisco Sánchez conducted another study (2006) which showed that out of 78 minors in a drug addiction rehabilitation program, 13 per cent inhaled solvent substances regularly. In the same way, researcher William Quevedo found that out of 343 individuals in a rehabilitation program (2007), 17 per cent had used inhalable substances different from gasoline and industrial glues. Ulises Cruz, from the Colombian Therapeutic Community, states that almost 100 per cent of the population he treats started using drugs between ages 12 and 13, which makes their recovery much more complex. “When consumption starts later in life (after age 18), it is easier for a person to quit their addiction. Early consumption start makes it more difficult to break dependency”, he explained. Health deterioration Such as with cocaine and other illegal drugs, the problem with solvents is that they cause irreversible health damage, with probability of rehabilitating close to zero. “They generate long term problems, such as a low in the immune system and lung damage; this because the solvent causes a sort of melting effect of the fat coating neurons and lungs which generates an irreversible anatomic alteration”, Tellez indicated. The root of the problem Many of the minors abusing drugs have done so because of lack of affection and communication. In other words, their affective void is the answer to the origin of their addiction. This is, according to Cruz and Téllez, evidence that the problem of drug addiction in children has its underlying cause in family distant relationships. “Here we have cases of parents who only found out about their children’s addiction problem 13 or 14 years after it started”, said Cruz. What to do with solvents? Science has made it clear that solvents are responsible for a percentage of drug addiction in minors in the country. This causes a dilemma in terms of what actions to take given that a large portion of industry is based on solvents, constituting a national economic support. “Solvents are fundamental for the technological-industrial development of the world. If we dispensed with them, how would many productive processes be conducted? This poses an ethical and practical dilemma”, said the Head of the Toxicology Department. “Benzene use is forbidden. It should not be used in any product”, emphasized Professor Osorno in reference to what Professor Tellez states is unfortunately still the case in many laboratories. Likewise, professionals point out the importance of parents establishing an affective connection with their children; that way it would be easier to identify the addiction problem from the beginning and treat it in time. But they emphasize that parents should talk to their children about narcotics and what they can cause before the minors may become drug addicts. Hernando Bernal form the Narcotics National Directorate Drug Observatory declares that change should be pursued in the formulas used by industries. “The idea is to find inert substances that are repelled by the human olfactory system because of their smell. The greatest problem we presently have is that the smell of these products is so pleasant that children are drawn towards taking them closer to their noses”. While this is accomplished, Professor Téllez asserts that “what is most pressing is to educate the general public and not hide a problem that has devastating consequences. (…) professors need to know more about this topic. Places where children with drug addiction problems can find support are very rare”.
