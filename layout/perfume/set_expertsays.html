The Expert Says

It Smells like a Thriller

Perfume: The Story of a Murderer, a cinematographic adaptation of the German novel Perfume, published in 1985 by Patrick Süskind, narrates the story of Jean-Baptiste Grenouille (Ben Whishaw), a character with a deep inner conflict who seeks recognition at all cost. Tykwer’s film, more than the story of a murderer with an obsession of preserving the most sublime and pure human fragrance, is a deep reverie about memories, power and beauty. Let’s remember that Tykwer has his own essential obsessions that loom in narrative time, the postmodern look and the structure of the thrillers. This cinematographic piece, framed in the France of the XVIII Century, transits between the modern age and the contemporary one, also known as the Century of Lights. It has an elliptical structure which uses permanent cliffhangers, narrative resource of thrillers which permanently powers the intrigue. The story centers on Grenouille, who is born inside the marketplace, the smelliest, most putrid place in Paris in 1783 and who is gifted with superior smell. This species of wild child travels through the evanescent realm of scents, a path which marks the mission of this youth and reveals the secrets of the mysterious method denominated enfleurage (the art of capturing and preserving the essence of the living beings). The film, filled with allegories and paradoxes, plants a first dilemma when the young Frenchman perceives and relates through smell, dissects the world in which he lives through smells and stinks, but lacks his own smell. He’s prevented from recognizing his own scent, as if it were about absolute lack of identity. Grenouille has a mission over his own life, to find the perfect perfume, the ideal fragrance. Tykwer assumes perfume as a metaphor; in this case the allusion is related to what the Greeks called Arke or the human essence; in words of Anaximens it is something like the air that gives origin to every being. Perfume as a synonym of purity, of divine exaltation; here we find another paradox that turns between divinity and monstrosity; he’s precisely the most abominable and horrid man who looks for acceptation and love in feminine beauty. It’s not a coincidence that the first (Karoline Herfurth) and last (Rachel Hurd-Wood) victims of Grenouille have similar characteristics: reddish hair, young virgins, delicate, and a look which permanently debates between divine and human, the sacred and profane, beauty and ugliness, antagonistic concepts but at the same time complimentary.
